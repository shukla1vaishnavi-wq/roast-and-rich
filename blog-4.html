Gen Z & Money

Why Gen Z struggles with money — and it's actually NOT our fault

📅 2026⏱ 5 min read🧠 Psychology

Every older generation loves to say "Gen Z wastes money on fancy cafés and avocado toast." (Or in India: "Gen Z wastes money on ordering food and new iPhone every year.")

And honestly? They're not entirely wrong. But they're also missing the why.

The system is actually rigged against us

Problem 1: Nobody taught us this stuff

Go check your Class 10, 11, 12 syllabus. How many chapters on personal finance? Zero. How many on compound interest in real life? Zero. How many on what a mutual fund is? Zero.

We learned Pythagoras theorem but not how to file taxes. We learned photosynthesis but not what an SIP is. The education system failed us completely on financial literacy.

😤 Hot take: Financial literacy should be a mandatory subject in Class 9. Until that changes, we're all just figuring it out on our own — which is exactly why apps like Roast&Rich exist.

Problem 2: UPI makes money feel fake

Remember when you'd physically count ₹500 notes before buying something? That resistance — the feel of real money leaving your hands — was a natural spending brake.

Now? You tap your phone. One second. Done. The transaction is so frictionless it barely registers as real money. Studies show people spend 20-30% more when paying digitally vs cash. UPI is incredible technology, but it broke our financial instincts.

Problem 3: Social media is a wealth illusion machine

Instagram, Reels, YouTube vlogs — everyone looks rich. That guy from your college is posting about his Europe trip. That girl is flexing a new phone every 8 months. That "entrepreneur" has a MacBook, AirPods, and a coffee shop aesthetic.

What you don't see: most of them are broke, borrowing from parents, or drowning in credit card debt. Social media shows the highlight reel. The FOMO it creates is real — and expensive.

Problem 4: Delayed gratification is harder than ever

Every app is designed to give you instant dopamine. Your orders arrive in hours, food in minutes, music instantly. Everything is now, now, now.

Saving money is the opposite. It's delayed gratification. You put money away today and feel nothing — the reward is 10 years away. Our brains, trained by the instant-delivery economy, genuinely struggle with this.

So what do we actually do about it?

Knowing the problem is half the solution. Here's the other half:

  • Make investing feel instant too — set up auto-SIP on salary day. Investment happens before you can spend it.
  • Bring back the "pain of paying" — use the Daily Tracker. Seeing numbers add up recreates the psychological friction that UPI removed.
  • Audit your FOMO, don't eliminate it — use the FOMO Filter before impulse buys. It doesn't say "never buy." It says "is this worth it right now?"
  • Unfollow 5 accounts that make you feel broke — your financial mood is real. Guard it.
💡 Key insight: You're not bad at money because you're irresponsible. You're bad at money because nobody taught you and the entire digital economy is optimized to take your money. That's not a personal failure. That's a systems problem — which you can personally fix.

You're reading this blog, which means you're already ahead of most people your age. That's not nothing. That's actually a big deal.
