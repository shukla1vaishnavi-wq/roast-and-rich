Mutual Funds

Mutual Funds, explained using a food–delivery app group order

📅 2026⏱ 5 min read🍱 Beginner

You know that moment when your friend group wants to order food, but the minimum order is ₹500 and none of you individually want to spend that much?

So you all put in ₹100-150 each, someone places the combined order, and everyone gets their portion of the food.

That's a mutual fund. Literally.

Okay but what actually IS a mutual fund?

A mutual fund is when a bunch of people (thousands of investors) pool their money together. A professional fund manager takes that combined pool and invests it across many stocks, bonds, or other assets.

Each person owns a proportional "unit" of the fund. When the investments grow, everyone's units become more valuable. When they fall, same — everyone shares the dip.

🍱 The food–delivery app analogy extended: Imagine the "fund manager" is the person who places the order. They know which dishes are good (which stocks are worth buying), they handle the whole order (portfolio management), and you just pay your share. When the food arrives (returns), everyone gets their proportional share based on how much they put in.

Why not just buy stocks directly?

You could! But buying individual stocks is hard for a few reasons:

  • You need a lot of money — one share of MRF costs ₹1.5 Lakh+. One unit of a mutual fund? ₹100.
  • You need to research constantly — which company is doing well? Which is about to crash? Fund managers do this full-time.
  • Diversification is expensive solo — spreading risk across 50 stocks takes enormous capital. A mutual fund does this automatically.

Types of Mutual Funds (simplified)

📈 Equity Funds

Invest mainly in stocks. Higher risk, higher potential returns (~12-15% over long term). Best for 5+ year goals. This is where most SIPs go.

🏛️ Debt Funds

Invest in government bonds, corporate bonds. Lower risk, lower returns (~6-8%). Best for 1-3 year goals where you can't afford to lose money.

⚖️ Hybrid / Balanced Funds

Mix of equity + debt. Medium risk, medium returns (~9-11%). Good middle ground for 3-5 year goals.

📊 Index Funds (our favourite)

Instead of a fund manager picking stocks, these just copy an index like Nifty 50. No guesswork. Lower fees (expense ratio). Historically, they beat most "active" fund managers over the long term. Start here.

What is NAV?

NAV = Net Asset Value. It's the price of one unit of the fund. Think of it as the "share price" of a mutual fund. If NAV is ₹50 and you invest ₹5000, you get 100 units. If NAV grows to ₹75, your 100 units are now worth ₹7500. Simple.

🧠 Direct vs Regular plans: Always choose "Direct Growth" when picking a mutual fund. Regular plans have a distributor commission baked in (typically 1% extra). Over 20 years, that 1% compounds into lakhs of rupees of difference. Direct = you skip the middleman = you keep more money.

TL;DR

Mutual fund = group order of investments. You put in a small amount, pros manage it, and you own a proportional share of a diversified portfolio. Start with a Nifty 50 Index Fund (Direct Growth). SIP ₹500/month. Thank yourself in 10 years.
