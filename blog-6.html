Finance 101

20 financial terms every Gen Z should know (explained without cringe)

📅 2026⏱ 7 min read📌 Save this

You're at your first job interview and the HR person says "your CTC includes variable pay and the HRA component." You nod confidently. You have no idea what any of that means.

We've all been there. Here's your no-BS cheat sheet for the 20 financial terms you'll actually use in real life.

SIP — Systematic Investment Plan
Auto-investing a fixed amount every month into a mutual fund.
Example: ₹1000 auto-deducted on the 5th of every month, invested in Nifty 50.
CTC — Cost To Company
The total amount a company spends on you per year. NOT what you take home. Includes salary + PF + gratuity + insurance.
Example: CTC ₹6LPA → actual in-hand might be ₹42-45k/month after deductions.
EMI — Equated Monthly Installment
Fixed monthly payment to repay a loan (home loan, car loan, personal loan). Includes both principal + interest.
Example: Phone costing ₹50,000 on 12-month EMI = roughly ₹4,600/month (with interest).
NAV — Net Asset Value
The price of one unit of a mutual fund. Like the "share price" of a fund. Calculated daily.
Example: NAV = ₹50 → ₹5000 investment = 100 units.
ELSS — Equity Linked Savings Scheme
A mutual fund that invests in stocks AND gives you tax deduction under Section 80C. 3-year lock-in.
Example: Invest ₹1.5L in ELSS → save up to ₹46,800 in taxes (at 30%).
FD — Fixed Deposit
Park money with a bank for a fixed period at a guaranteed interest rate. Safe but low returns (~6-7%).
Example: ₹50,000 in FD for 1 year at 7% = ₹53,500 at maturity.
PPF — Public Provident Fund
Government savings scheme. ~7.1% interest, 15-year lock-in. Tax-free returns. Great for long-term safe savings.
HRA — House Rent Allowance
Part of your salary meant for rent. If you pay rent, you can claim HRA exemption to reduce taxable income.
TDS — Tax Deducted at Source
Tax deducted automatically from your income (salary, FD interest, freelance payment) before you receive it.
ITR — Income Tax Return
The form you file with the government every year declaring your income, deductions, and taxes. Due July 31.
XIRR — Extended Internal Rate of Return
The actual annualized return on your investments (especially SIPs), accounting for irregular cash flows. Better than simple percentage return.
Expense Ratio
Annual fee charged by a mutual fund for managing your money. Expressed as a percentage. Lower is better. Direct funds have lower expense ratios than Regular funds.
Example: 0.1% expense ratio vs 1.5% — on ₹10 Lakh over 20 years, that difference is ₹20+ Lakh.
Portfolio
Your total collection of investments — stocks, mutual funds, FDs, gold, everything combined.
Compounding
When your returns also generate returns. Interest on interest. The snowball effect that makes long-term investing so powerful.
Example: ₹100 at 12% → ₹112 year 1 → ₹125.44 year 2 (not ₹124). The 44 paise extra is compounding.
Liquidity
How quickly you can convert an asset into cash without losing value. FD = low liquidity. Savings account = high liquidity.
Diversification
Spreading money across different types of investments so one crash doesn't destroy everything. "Don't put all eggs in one basket."
Bull Market / Bear Market
Bull = prices rising, economy positive, good time to invest. Bear = prices falling, economy negative, scary but actually great time for SIPs.
Inflation
The rate at which prices rise over time. India ~6-7%/year. If your savings earn less than inflation, you're losing real money.
Example: ₹100 today = ₹94 of purchasing power next year at 6% inflation. FD at 6% = barely keeping up.
Net Worth
Total assets (what you own) minus total liabilities (what you owe). Your real financial snapshot.
Example: Savings ₹50,000 + investments ₹1,00,000 − loan ₹30,000 = Net worth ₹1,20,000.
Emergency Fund
3-6 months of expenses kept in a liquid account (savings account or liquid MF) for unexpected situations — job loss, medical emergency, etc.
Example: Monthly expenses ₹15,000 → Emergency fund target: ₹45,000-90,000.
